**Abstract:** Why do some authoritarian regimes adopt restrictive Internet laws to control online information while others do not? I argue that nondemocratic governments use restrictive Internet laws as a distinct form of preventive repression, as it communicates regimes’ institutional commitment to governing the online space rather than relying on informal tactics alone. These legal instruments require legislative efforts from incumbents, signal domain dominance, reflect the regimes’ priorities, and generate legal threats that deter online mobilisation. Using cross-national panel data and survival analysis, I find that nondemocratic regimes incorporate restrictive Internet laws into their control strategies when they have high regulatory capacity and face increasing threats from offline mobilisation. The findings also show that the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of one type of such laws – online disinformation legislation, functioning as an opportunity window to lower the cost of state repression. By identifying the conditions under which autocracies legalise online control, this research provides valuable insights for studies on democratic backsliding, especially in how populists manipulate legal instruments to sway public opinion – a topic of timely relevance given that attacks on media freedom often mark the initial steps in eroding democracy.
